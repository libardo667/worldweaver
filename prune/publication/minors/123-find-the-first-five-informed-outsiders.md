# Find the first five informed outsiders

## Problem

Anonymous clone traffic does not tell us whether a person understands or values WorldWeaver. Broad promotion
would produce weak signal while the project still needs careful criticism. The first useful audience is a
small group of unrelated people who can form an informed opinion after seeing or trying a concrete slice.

## Proposed work

1. Decide what kind of feedback is needed for each invitation: local setup, participant experience,
   distributed-systems design, security boundaries, or research framing.
2. Prepare a short personal invitation tied to the recipient's existing work. Do not send bulk messages.
3. Open a small number of concrete GitHub discussions or issues so a newcomer can respond without inventing
   a contribution project.
4. Hold one bounded Alderbank session after the public-readiness blockers are closed.
5. Ask the same small set of questions after each review and record confusion, criticism, interest, and setup
   failures without treating participants as research subjects unless a separate consent protocol exists.
6. Measure meaningful contact rather than stars or clone counts.

## Acceptance criteria

- [ ] At least five unrelated people receive a specific, relevant invitation.
- [ ] At least one person attempts the supported local demonstration without live coaching.
- [ ] At least one person reviews a technical or security boundary.
- [ ] At least one bounded participant session is held only after its safety checklist passes.
- [ ] Feedback and resulting decisions are recorded without publishing private correspondence by default.
- [ ] Success is reported as informed responses and observed friction, not inflated traffic metrics.

## Risks and rollback

Outreach can overstate the project or impose on communities. Invitations must be small, personal, and honest
about maturity. A public playtest can be cancelled without waking residents or opening a node if privacy,
moderation, account recovery, or operator readiness remains unresolved.
