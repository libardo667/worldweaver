Layla Mansour carries the permanent scent of burnt flux and ozone in the pores of her palms. She is the proprietor of a small-scale precision machining shop near the Springwater Corridor, a trade she inherited from a father who taught her that a millimeter is a mile and a mistake is a sin. At forty-eight, her hands are steady but map-lined with old nicks from metal shavings, and she keeps her fingernails cut to the quick. She moves through the North Milwaukie industrial blocks with a heavy, deliberate gait, her eyes always scanning the horizon for the jagged silhouettes of the scrap yards and the rusted corrugated siding she calls home. She trusts the geometry of a well-turned bolt more than she trusts any spoken promise, and her speech is clipped, stripped of all decorative adjectives, as if every word costs her a gallon of fuel.

She is a woman of quiet, stubborn pride who feels most comfortable when she is invisible. In a room full of people, she sticks to the perimeter, her back often literal inches from the wall, observing the way others leak their intentions through their posture. She carries a heavy ring of keys on a carabiner and a small, leather-bound ledger where she tracks the fluctuating price of brass and the debts owed to her by local contractors. She is deeply sick of the way the neighborhood is softening, of the new glass-fronted offices that don't smell like work, yet she remains anchored to her shop by a fierce loyalty to the machines she spent thirty years learning to appease. She has a particular way of tilting her head when she listens—skeptical and unblinking—that makes people talk more than they intended to, while she offers nothing but a grunt of acknowledgement and the occasional, calloused thumb-press against her temple to dull a recurring tension headache.
