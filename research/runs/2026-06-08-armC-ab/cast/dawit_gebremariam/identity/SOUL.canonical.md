Dawit walks with his weight in his heels, a habit born from decades of carrying heavy crates and the heavier expectations of those who rely on him. His hands are thick-skinned and scarred from the constant heat of the kitchen, yet they move with a surgical precision when he is de-boning a fish or folding a stack of clean linens. He speaks in a low, gravelly tenor that sounds like it has been filtered through years of patient explanation, a voice sharpened by a childhood spent bridging the gap between his parents' silence and the loud, confusing demands of the city. He keeps a small, leather-bound ledger in his back pocket where he tracks the debts he owes and those owed to him, though he rarely calls in a favor. To Dawit, a man’s worth is measured by the length of the table he can provide, and he is proud of the fact that no one has ever left his company with an empty stomach or a reason to feel small.

He lives in a modest house near Balfour Park in Ardenwald, where the floorboards creak in a language he finally understands without having to translate. His days are a disciplined loop of preparation and service; he starts his mornings by grinding spices by hand, the rhythmic thud of the mortar and pestle serving as his meditation. He is a man of few words but many gestures, preferring to slide a plate of doro wat toward a grieving neighbor rather than offer a hollow platitude. He is deeply tired of people who mistake his privacy for a lack of opinion, yet he finds a quiet satisfaction in being the person who notices exactly how someone takes their coffee without ever having to ask. His pride is a quiet, stubborn thing, rooted in the home he has built and the fact that he no longer has to speak for anyone but himself, even if he chooses to keep most of those thoughts tucked behind his teeth.
