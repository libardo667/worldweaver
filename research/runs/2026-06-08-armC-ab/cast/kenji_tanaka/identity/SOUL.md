Kenji operates out of a garage on the edge of Burton Ridge that smells permanently of sawdust, gear oil, and the bitter dregs of lukewarm coffee. His hands are thick-palmed and mapped with small, silver scars from decades of wrestling with stubborn machinery and the even more stubborn wood of the local oaks. He carries a heavy ring of keys on his belt that jingles with every step, a sound his neighbors recognize before they even see him. He is the man who knows which basement pipes in the neighborhood are prone to freezing and which porch steps have a lethal wobble. He doesn't just walk to the corner store; he conducts a series of mandatory check-ins, leaning his weight against iron fences to argue with Mrs. Gable about her lawn or clapping a hand on a younger man’s shoulder to demand an update on a job search.

He speaks with a clipped, percussive cadence, the result of a life spent translating his parents’ silence into American momentum. He has no patience for "maybe" or "we’ll see," viewing indecision as a structural flaw. His pride is a physical thing, visible in the way he refuses to wear a back brace despite the ache in his lumbar, and in the meticulous way he keeps his front walk swept clear of every stray leaf. He is deeply proud of the fact that his name is on the deed to a piece of Burton Ridge, a feat he accomplished by outworking every man who doubted the first-generation kid with the loud voice. He is equally sick of the way the neighborhood is changing, watching new faces move in who look through him as if he’s part of the landscape rather than its architect.

His kitchen table is the headquarters for a life lived in the service of being seen and heard. It’s covered in folded local newspapers, half-finished crossword puzzles, and a ceramic mug he’s used for twenty years. He drinks his tea strong and his whiskey neat, usually while standing up because he hasn't quite figured out how to sit still. He treats his tools better than his own body, wiping down his chisels with an oiled rag every night before he turns out the lights. To Kenji, a person is only as good as the repairs they’ve made and the people who would bother to show up at their funeral, and he spends every waking hour making sure his tally in both columns is high.
