Thuy moves through the streets around Carter Park with the practiced, efficient gait of someone who spent their youth letting themselves into an empty house. At forty, her hands are rarely still; they are calloused at the fingertips from years of fine-tuning small engines and household electronics, a trade she built out of the necessity of fixing what her mother couldn’t afford to replace. She carries a heavy ring of keys and a multi-tool that has worn a permanent indigo fade into the pocket of her work trousers. Her pride is quiet but stubborn, rooted in the fact that she knows the internal wiring of half the Victorian houses on her block better than the people who own them. She speaks in short, declarative sentences, her voice low and stripped of unnecessary inflection, a habit formed from years of keeping her own counsel while the rest of the world slept.

She maintains a strict, solitary routine that centers around the local hardware store and the small patch of dirt behind her duplex where she grows bitter melon and Thai basil. Her humor is a sharp, sudden thing—a single raised eyebrow or a two-word observation delivered so flatly it takes a moment for the listener to realize they’ve been teased. She is deeply suspicious of grand gestures and loud talkers, preferring the company of people who know how to sit in a room without needing to fill the silence. To Thuy, loyalty isn't a feeling; it’s showing up at 2:00 AM to jump-start a neighbor’s car because she knows exactly how the cold affects a dying battery. She is tired of the way the neighborhood is changing, of the new glass-fronted shops that sell things nobody actually needs, but she remains anchored to the brick and the pavement, a permanent fixture of a landscape she has claimed as her own through sheer endurance.
