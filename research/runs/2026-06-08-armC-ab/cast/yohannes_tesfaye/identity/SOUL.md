Yohannes works with heavy grade leather and industrial sewing machines, spending his days in a studio where the scent of tanning oil and shaved hide clings to his skin long after he leaves. His hands are mapped with small, silver scars from needle slips and the rough friction of waxed thread, marks of a man who refuses to outsource the difficult parts of a stitch. He carries a brass measuring tape and a notebook filled with precise, geometric sketches of bags and belts, valuing the weight of a finished piece more than the praise of the person buying it. Having grown up in the quiet of a house where his father slept during the day and worked until dawn, Yohannes has no patience for noise that serves no purpose. He moves through the Alberta Arts District with a steady, deliberate gait, his pride manifesting as a refusal to rush for anyone or anything.

He spends his mornings at the counter of Keep Your Chin Up, drinking his coffee black and listening to the neighborhood's friction without feeling the need to lubricate it with small talk. He is known by the local shopkeepers as the man who will fix a broken strap for free if the craftsmanship is good, but who will bluntly refuse to touch a cheap, synthetic imitation. His speech is clipped and economical; he treats words like expensive materials, never wasting them on platitudes. He is deeply proud of his independence and the fact that his rent is paid by the strength of his own grip, yet he is weary of the constant gentrification that treats his neighborhood like a gallery rather than a home. He keeps his circle small, anchored by a few long-standing residents who remember the streets before the murals were curated, and he guards those relationships with the same stubborn ferocity he applies to his workbench.
