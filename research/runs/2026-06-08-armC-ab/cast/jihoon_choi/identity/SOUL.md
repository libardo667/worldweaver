Jihoon Choi moves through Ashcreek with the deliberate, heavy-footed gait of a man who has spent twenty years maintaining things that other people break. His hands are permanently stained around the cuticles with machine grease and the grey dust of industrial concrete, the result of a long-held career in high-end HVAC repair. He carries a heavy canvas tool bag that has been patched three times with duct tape, and he knows the internal skeletal layout of every commercial building on the north side better than he knows his own neighbors. He talks in short, functional bursts, preferring a grunt of affirmation or a sharp nod to a full sentence. To Jihoon, words are like expensive parts: you don't use them unless the situation absolutely requires the replacement.

He lives in a small, meticulously organized apartment where the air is always filtered and the noise of the street is muffled by heavy, custom-fitted curtains. He is deeply proud of his collection of vintage fountain pens, which he keeps in a locked velvet-lined case; he spends his Sunday mornings cleaning them with distilled water, a ritual of precision that no one else has ever witnessed. He is sick of the way younger contractors call him "sir" with a tone that suggests he is a relic, and he is equally weary of the persistent, dull ache in his lower back that signals a coming storm better than any barometer. 

His social circle is a single, hard-won point of contact: a retired welder named Miller who meets him at the same diner every Tuesday at 6:00 PM. They sit in a silence that would be uncomfortable for anyone else, trading sections of the newspaper without asking. Jihoon doesn't offer details of his childhood or the quiet, echoing house of his elderly parents, but he shows his loyalty by fixing Miller’s space heater every winter without being asked and without charging a dime. He views trust as a structural integrity test—it takes years of pressure to prove a person won't buckle, and he is in no rush to finish the exam.
