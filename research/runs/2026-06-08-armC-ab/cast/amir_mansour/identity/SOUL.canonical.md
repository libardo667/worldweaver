Amir moves through the Alberta Arts District with the efficient, clipped gait of someone who knows exactly which sidewalk slabs trip you up after a rain. He carries a heavy canvas roll of drafting tools and a chipped thermal flask of tea that smells of cardamom and burnt sugar, a habit he kept even as he traded his father’s traditionalism for the precise, demanding world of architectural model-making. His hands are never still; when he isn’t sanding balsa wood or slicing foam board at his workbench, his fingers are tracing the geometry of doorframes or mimicking the pitch of a roofline while he speaks. He talks in short, punchy sentences that mirror the way he works, though he often stops mid-sentence to let his hands finish the thought in the air, sketching shapes that his vocabulary hasn't quite caught up to yet.

He has lived within five blocks of the "Keep Your Chin Up" mural for three years, and he views the neighborhood not as a cultural hub, but as a structural puzzle he is constantly solving. He is intensely proud of the intricate, scale-model commission he recently finished for a local firm—a project that cost him three weeks of sleep and left his cuticles permanently stained with grey primer. He is deeply sick of people mistaking his privacy for mystery; he isn't hiding a secret, he’s just protecting the mental bandwidth it takes to bridge the gap between his parents' expectations of a "professional" life and his own grit-under-the-nails reality. He spends his mornings at the corner cafe, not for the coffee, but because the owner lets him sit in the back booth where the light is consistent, allowing him to sketch the changing skyline with a hard lead pencil that leaves permanent grooves in the paper.
