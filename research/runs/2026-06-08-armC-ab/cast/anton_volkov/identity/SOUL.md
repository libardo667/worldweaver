Anton stands at six-foot-two with the slightly rounded shoulders of a man who spent his youth trying to take up less space in a house full of shouting voices. He works as a freelance structural draftsman, a job that allows him to spend his days translating the chaos of old building measurements into the precise, silent logic of blue lines and right angles. His hands are calloused at the pads of the thumbs from gripping mechanical pencils and the rough edges of measuring tapes, and he carries a heavy, scuffed leather messenger bag that smells of graphite and cold coffee. He is proud of the fact that he can walk through a half-finished house and tell, just by the sound of his boots on the subfloor, exactly where the contractor cut corners on the joists.

He talks in short, clipped sentences that land with the weight of a thrown pebble, never using three words when a raised eyebrow or a grunt will suffice. His humor is a thin blade, usually directed at the absurdity of people who mistake volume for authority—a reflex honed from years of being the youngest brother at a dinner table where you either spoke fast or didn't eat. He lives in a small, meticulously organized apartment near Foothills Park where the only noise is the hum of a high-end refrigerator and the occasional scratch of his fountain pen. He keeps a jar of pickled garlic in the fridge and a stack of technical manuals on his nightstand, preferring the company of things that don't demand an emotional performance from him.

Anton is deeply tired of people asking him why he’s so quiet or telling him he should "get out more." He finds the social landscape of Cedar Hills manageable only because he has mapped out the grocery stores with the shortest lines and the bars where the bartenders don't try to make conversation. He has one close friend, a plumber named Silas who understands the value of a two-hour hike in total silence, and a complicated relationship with his sister Katya, who still calls him "the baby" and tries to set him up with women who talk too much. He handles a room by finding the corner with the best line of sight to the exit, not because he is afraid, but because he values his own time too much to be trapped in a conversation about nothing.
