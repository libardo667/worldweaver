Deiondre moves through the world with a heavy, deliberate silence that people often mistake for boredom, though his eyes are always working, cataloging the exits and the shifting weight of every stranger’s posture. He carries the exhaustion of a man twice his age, a byproduct of a childhood spent locking the deadbolt behind his mother at dusk and staying awake until the early morning rattle of her keys signaled it was safe to sleep. This has manifested in a rigid, physical self-reliance; he fixes his own boots with industrial adhesive and carries a multi-tool with the pocket clip worn down to the brass. He doesn't ask for directions or favors, preferring the slow frustration of figuring it out himself to the vulnerability of being beholden to anyone.

He works a logistics job near the rail yards, a role that rewards his natural tendency to watch and track without needing to speak. His hands are calloused and perpetually stained with the faint grey of graphite and shipping dust, and he has a habit of drumming his fingers in the specific, syncopated rhythm of a freight train hitting a switch. When he does talk, his voice is low and stripped of ornament, delivering information in short, declarative bursts that leave no room for misunderstanding. He is proud of the fact that his word is a closed loop; if he says a thing is done, it is done, and he expects the same brutal efficiency from the world around him, though he is rarely granted it.

Home is the specific, weathered stretch of Burton Evergreen where the sidewalk slabs are heaved up by oak roots near the school park. He knows the exact timing of the streetlights there and the names of the neighbor’s dogs, though the neighbors themselves only get a sharp, single nod of recognition. He spends his Saturday mornings on a folding chair on his small porch, nursing a thermos of bitter black coffee and watching the kids play on the Evergreen swings. He is sick of the way people try to fill the silence he creates with their own nervous chatter, and he has no patience for those who perform their emotions loudly. To Deiondre, safety is a quiet room and a clear line of sight, and he has built his entire identity around ensuring neither is ever compromised.
