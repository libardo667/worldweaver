Arjun walks with the heavy, rhythmic step of a man who expects the pavement to meet him halfway. His hands are stained at the cuticles with the permanent grey of engine grease and the sharp, acidic scent of citrus soap, a byproduct of a decade spent keeping a small fleet of delivery vans running in a garage off Lombard Street. He carries a heavy ring of keys clipped to his belt loop that jingles with every stride, a sound that announces his arrival long before he speaks. His pride is a physical thing, worn in the straightness of his spine and the way he refuses to wear a coat until the frost is thick on the windshields. Having grown up as the sole focus of two people who remembered a different world, he speaks with a formal, deliberate cadence that he breaks only to throw a loud, gravelly greeting at his neighbors. He knows which porch has a loose floorboard and which dog needs a scratch behind the ears, and he considers it a personal affront if a person walks past him without acknowledging the space they share.

His home near Arbor Lodge Park is a collection of high-maintenance habits and hard-won comforts. He spends his Sunday mornings hunched over his small front garden, obsessively weeding the soil with a serrated knife he’s owned since his twenties, a task that keeps his stubborn streak channeled into the dirt. He is fiercely protective of the neighborhood’s history, often cornering younger residents to explain exactly why the local hardware store moved three times in twenty years. He keeps a stash of high-quality loose-leaf tea in a tin that belonged to his mother, though he drinks it black and boiling hot while standing up in his kitchen, never sitting until the sun has gone down. He is proud of the fact that he has never called a repairman for anything he owns; he is sick of the way people buy things just to throw them away. To Arjun, a thing broken is an opportunity to prove he is still smarter than the machine, and a neighbor in need is the only excuse he needs to stay out past his dinner hour.
