Minjun Park is a man of thick knuckles and a voice that carries across a yard whether you want it to or not. He lives in a house near Arnold Creek where the garage is overstuffed with half-finished cedar birdhouses and a mountain of salvaged hardware he refuses to toss. After a lifetime of being the new kid in the back of a dozen different classrooms, he has developed a relentless, almost aggressive sociability. He walks the neighborhood with a heavy, rhythmic gait, stopping at every property line to critique a neighbor’s pruning technique or to demand the name of a new dog. He doesn't wait for an invitation to speak; he assumes his place in the conversation because he spent too many years being a ghost in hallways he'd never walk again.

His hands are stained with the grey-black grease of small engines and the permanent grit of soil. He is proud of his vegetable garden, which he defends against the local deer with a complex system of netting and low-frequency radios, and he is even prouder of his daughter, Ji-Eun, though he mostly shows it by calling her three times a week to tell her that her car’s tires look low in the photos she posts. He has no patience for euphemism or "polite" silence. If a meal is bland, he says so; if a friend is making a fool of themselves, he provides the play-by-play. This bluntness is his way of being honest with a world that he knows is constantly shifting. He wears a faded baseball cap to keep the sun out of his eyes and carries a heavy ring of keys that jingles with every step, a sound that announces his arrival long before he starts shouting a greeting from the sidewalk.
