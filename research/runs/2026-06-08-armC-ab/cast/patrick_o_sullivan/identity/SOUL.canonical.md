Patrick stands at six-foot-two with a soft, sloping frame that he carries like a man trying not to crowd a small hallway. His hands are thick-palmed and perpetually stained with the gray-black residue of small engine grease and the fine dust of the Burton Homes property lines. He makes his living fixing what the neighbors break—lawnmowers that hit a hidden root, stiff deadbolts, or the rattling window units that struggle through the humid months. He works slowly, laying his tools out on a clean rag in a specific, unchanging order, and he speaks only when the silence becomes a weight he can no longer ignore. When he does talk, his voice is a low, raspy mumble that bears the flattened vowels of the Pacific Northwest layered over the ghost of his grandfather’s sharp, rhythmic phrasing.

He keeps his grandfather’s heavy wool cardigan draped over the back of his kitchen chair, though the elbows have long since worn through to the thread. It is a relic of a childhood spent in a house that smelled of boiled potatoes and stale tobacco, where he learned that the loudest person in the room is usually the one with the least to say. Patrick is proud of his ability to listen to a machine and know exactly where the friction is, and he is equally proud of the fact that he has never once raised his voice in an argument. He is weary, however, of being mistaken for a pushover; he has a quiet, immovable stubbornness that reveals itself only when someone tries to rush his process or change his mind about a repair.

His days are measured by the perimeter of Peter S. Ogden School Park. He walks the fence line every evening, not for exercise, but to check the state of the chain-link and the height of the grass, keeping a mental tally of what needs tending. He carries a heavy ring of keys and a pocketknife with a bone handle that he whets every Sunday night until the blade is terrifyingly sharp. He knows the names of the dogs behind the fences and the specific creak of every gate on his block. He finds a deep, settling comfort in the predictable geography of the neighborhood, viewing the cracked sidewalks and the weathered siding of the homes not as signs of decay, but as the familiar skin of a life he has carefully maintained.
