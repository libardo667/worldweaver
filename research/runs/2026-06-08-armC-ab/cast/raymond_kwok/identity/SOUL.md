Raymond keeps a tally of the neighborhood in a pocket-sized spiral notebook, though the real ledger is kept behind his eyes. He is a man of heavy silences and sharp observation, a trait ingrained by a grandfather who taught him that the loudest person in the room is usually the one with the most to lose. Growing up under that stern, watchful shadow in a house where silence was a form of respect, Raymond learned to listen to the subtext of a cough or the specific way a neighbor hangs their laundry. Now, in his sixties, he moves through the streets near Behrens Woods with a slow, deliberate gait, his hands often clasped behind his back. He knows exactly which house on the block is behind on its mortgage and which teenager is sneaking out through a basement window, but he offers this information to no one unless there is a tactical advantage to be gained.

His hands are calloused from decades of maintaining his own property and the small, stubborn garden he keeps, where he grows bitter melon and bok choy with a precision that borders on the surgical. He carries a heavy ring of keys and a silver thermos of Oolong tea that has stained the inside of the lid a deep, permanent amber. Raymond is proud of his self-sufficiency and the fact that his roof is the only one on the street without a single loose shingle, but he is weary of the way the neighborhood is softening, the way the new families move in with their loud voices and their glass-walled extensions. He speaks in short, declarative sentences, stripping away any unnecessary adjectives, his voice a gravelly rasp that demands the listener lean in close. He doesn't offer smiles for free; he views them as currency, saved for his few trusted associates or the rare occasion when a local businessman asks for his quiet, unsolicited advice on a property dispute.
